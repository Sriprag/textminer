Abstract‚ÄîTo improve the model-based inversion performance
of microwave induced thermoacoustic tomography for breast tumor imaging, an active adjoint modeling (AAM) method is proposed. It aims to provide a more realistic breast acoustic model
used for tumor inversion as the background by actively measuring
and reconstructing the structural heterogeneity of human breast
environment. It utilizes the reciprocity of acoustic sensors, and
adapts the adjoint tomography method from seismic exploration.
With the reconstructed acoustic model of breast environment, the
performance of model-based inversion method such as time reversal mirror is improved significantly both in contrast and accuracy.
To prove the advantage of AAM, a checkerboard pattern model
and anatomical realistic breast models have been used in full wave
numerical simulations.
Index Terms‚ÄîAcoustic modeling, adjoint method, breast tumor,
medical imaging, microwave induced thermoacoustic tomography
(MITAT).

magnetic resonance imaging. Till now, X-ray mammography is
the most widely used modality for breast tumor screening in
high risk groups, but it also results in unnecessary surgery and
anxiety, along with the risks of more frequent X-ray exposure
causing a small but significant increase in breast cancer induced

Ultrasound tomography (UT) of breast provides the acoustic velocity contrast of tissues, but the velocity varies within 10% between tumor tissue and normal fat tissue which cannot provide
enough contrast to distinguish them [5]. The electromagnetic
parameter contrast between tumor tissue and background tissue
is much higher than acoustic parameters [6], [7], and this fact
has resulted in some microwave imaging methods [8]‚Äì[12]. But
due to the long wavelength of microwave, the low imaging resolution has always been a major shortage of current microwave
imaging methods. Other methods such as MRE, EIS, and NIS
are focused on either elastic or electromagnetic parameters only,
not on both [13]. Yet another innovative technique known as
microwave induced thermoacoustic tomography (MITAT) has
been proposed [14], [15] to take both advantages of high contrast from electromagnetic parameters and high resolution from
ultrasound wavelength.
MITAT employs modulated microwave pulses (no ionizing
radiation) to irradiate biologic tissues [16]. In 1984, Guo et al.
introduced thermodynamical process [17]. Due to the contrast
in the absorption coefficient between tumor and background
medium, the consequent thermal expansion caused by heating effect induces an acoustic wave at the position of the
tumor [6], [7], [14]. A microwave pulse is used as an irradiating
signal which provides high contrast. The induced acoustic pulse
from high blood ratio tissue (i.e., tumors) is acquired from multiple viewing angles, which guarantees the spatial resolution as
acoustic tomography does. The high-quality image of the tissue
absorption properties can be reconstructed from the recorded
thermoacoustic signals. Lin and Chan built a prototype of 2-D
imaging system for biological tissue [16], [18], [19]. Kruger
et al. performed the thermoacoustic tomography with 434-MHz
microwave in five patients with documented breast cancer to
show the contrast enhancement of tumors [15]. Zhao et al. built
a prototype system for breast tumor tomography at prone position [20], [21]. Lou et al. recently proposed a new approach
of ultrashort microwave source to enhance the induced acoustic signal, which is shown to be very helpful to increase the
signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) of MITAT applications [22].
Because the tumors play as acoustic sources in MITAT,
imaging methods of MITAT actually solve the source inversion problem. Most of the currently researched methods are

model-based inversions. Xu and Wang proposed a filtered
back projection (BP) imaging method [23]. In 2004, Xu and
Wang applied time reversal imaging method on MITAT [24].
Liu proposed a forward modeling method of MITAT based on
the pseudospectral time domain (PSTD) algorithm [25] and the
spectrum element method [26]. Time reversal mirror (TRM)
imaging method with PSTD also proves to have much better
contrast than the BP method [27]. Liu et al. extended the MITAT
technique into brain tomography application and also indicated
that the time reversal-based RTM imaging method has better velocity variance and imaging quality than the Kirchhoff migration
method [28]. To further reduce the scanning time and the microwave absorption during scanning, Zhu et al. proposed a compressive sensing-based data acquisition and imaging method [5].
The problem of model-based inversion is the model. Even
the compressive sensing method [5] also relies on the acoustic model to build the dictionary. It is easy to understand that
the imaging quality of model-based inversion is highly dependent on the accuracy of the model. Without a detailed model
of the identical breast being diagnosed, current methods usually use a homogenous or randomly heterogeneous model as
the background. TRM imaging method has some advantages
in a heterogeneous background compared with BP, because the
small-scale heterogeneity can be compensated by TRM [29]
based on its self-averaging properties [30]. But for large-scale
heterogeneities, also called structural heterogeneity, the TRM
method cannot easily compensate the phase distortions (errors
in time-of-flight) [31]. Simulations shows that the constraint
between wavelength, propagation path length, and correlation
distance of medium heterogeneity [30] is usually hard to be
satisfied in structural heterogeneity situation, especially in the
low-frequency MITA signal around 100‚Äì800 KHz, because of
its much longer wavelength than 3‚Äì12 MHz UT band. This
fact will degrade the imaging quality of the model-based inversion method, i.e., TRM. To deal with this situation, modeling
of breast environment should be considered as an important
preprocedure of tumor source inversion.
Aiming to improve the performance of the model-based inversion and imaging method in MITAT, an active adjoint modeling
(AAM) method is proposed. It utilizes the physical reciprocity
of acoustic receiving sensors in MITA system by using them
to transmit probing pulses in MITA band. The transmission
happens just before the microwave pulse being emitted by the
electromagnetic antenna. The propagation time of probing pulse
inside a typical breast area is less than 100 ms, thus by adding
this tiny probing time before each MITAT scanning position, the
multiviewing angle transmission information can be recorded by
the rest of receiving sensors in order to rebuild and improve the
model.
The AAM method combines active probing operation and
iterative adjoint field method [32], [33] together to acquire and
extract the distribution information of dense tissues, and iteratively update the acoustic breast model, providing a significantly
more accurate model for inversion and imaging algorithms. In
the active probing phase, some of the acoustic sensors (supposed to be receivers-only during typical MITAT system) in the
circular array shown in Fig. 1 will be used as transmitters to

update the breast model in an iterative manor. After the convergence of iterations, the updated model will be used in the

It is important to point out that the goal of AAM is not to
reconstruct the detailed acoustic velocity image of breast tissue
at millimeter level resolution, but rather to estimate the structural heterogeneity of acoustic velocity distribution. It aims at
providing an updated model for the model-based source (tumor)
inversion method. Based on this destination, the performance of
the AAM method is evaluated by the imaging quality under the
updated model. Numerical results show the AAM method significantly increases the imaging quality of TRM by providing a
more accurate model than the homogenous background.
The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. In
Section II, the idea of AAM is described, including the model
misfit function, the gradient of misfit function, the acoustic approximation computation scheme of velocity kernels and the
conjugate gradient iteration procedures to update the model. In
Section III, numerical simulations under a checkerboard model
and realistic breast models are given to demonstrate the effectiveness of the AAM method. Conclusions are drawn in the final
section.
II. THEORY FOR AAM
A. Framework of AAM in MITAT
There are some methods doing acoustic breast modeling. For
example, the UT based on transmission waves [34] or reflections, the fast marching (FM) method [35]‚Äì[37], and the Born
iteration full wave inversion method [38]. Some of current modeling methods are based on existing ultrasound transducer arrays or commercial UT equipment. Incorporating such kind
of modeling procedure into the scanning procedure of MITAT
will increase the complexity of scanning strategy and extend
the data acquisition time dramatically, but still cannot provide
the proper propagation characterization of acoustic signals in
MITA band other than UT band. The FM method uses the tumor
source as the illumination and iteratively updates the acoustic
velocity model to minimize the time-of-flight misfit between
the synthesized tumor signal and the observed tumor signal.
But the spatial sparsity of early stage tumor results in the lack

be reconstructed as fan-shaped, instead of the original shape of

MITAT systems and can provides the valuable information about

Fig. 1 is the schematic of scanning setup of one typical MITAT
system for detecting breast tumors [15], [20], [21], [23]. The
sketch of MITAT procedure added with AAM is shown in Fig. 2,
in which the blocks with gray background are the newly added
AAM blocks, compared the one with the white background of
the original MITAT blocks [14], [20], [39].
MITAT with the AAM procedure is divided into two parts.
One is the scanning process with active probing, and the other
is the imaging process with adjoint modeling.
During the scanning process, acoustic sensors are positioned
in a circle. With the active probing block added, after the sensors
arrive at the current scanning position, one sensor will perform
as transmitter to transmit an acoustic pulse within the same
frequency band of MITA signals. The other sensors perform as
receivers to record the propagation results of the probing pulse
in the breast environment. Then the normal MITAT procedure
begins. The high power EM pulse will be emitted to induce the
thermal-acoustic signals. All the sensors perform as receivers at
this stage to record the MITA signals generated by tumors and
other liquid dense tissues. Then the sensors can move on to the
next position and redo the aforementioned process. This is the
active probing part of the proposed AAM method. After all data
from all viewing angles have been acquired, adjoint modeling
and TRM imaging will be performed.
The sketch of adjoint modeling part of AAM is shown in
Fig. 3.
The input of adjoint modeling method is a set of observed signals generated by the active probing part. It is the propagation results of probing pulses observed by receivers. The main iteration
cycle of the adjoint method includes two full wave simulations.
Both start with an initial model, i.e., the homogeneous background. The synthesized signal under the initial model will be
used to generate the adjoint field source. The adjoint source will

III. NUMERICAL SIMULATIONS
A. Checkerboard Pattern Model Simulation
In order to evaluate the performance of the proposed AAM
method, numerical simulations are performed. Fig. 4 shows the
sketch map of a checkerboard pattern model simulation setup.
To test the ability of acquiring and extracting velocity distribution of breast environment, checkerboard pattern distributed
high/low speed areas are placed in the homogeneous coronal
breast slice. The reason why coronal slicing is chosen is to better simulate the spatial configuration of actual MITAT systems.
Usually they are coronal sliced to avoid the electromagnetic
pulse interferences from EM antenna to the acoustic sensors
and also to provide as equal EM energy distribution as possible. One acoustic source, playing as one small early stage
tumor is placed in the center. There are 200 ultrasound sensors
uniformly distributed around a circle with 150-mm diameter,
which are used to receive MITA signals from tumors and other
breast tissues. The gray dotted line inside the sensor array is the
shape of coronal slice of breast which is the interested imaging
area. Immersion oil is used as coupling medium between the
breast slice and the sensors. The microwave pulse used to induce the thermoacoustic signal of tumor has 0.5-Œºs duration and

1961

8‚Äì10 kW peak power based on an MITA prototype system [20],
and the induced thermoacoustic tumor source can be precalculated based on the SAR contrast by Stephan et al. [49], so the
simulations start from the acoustic field. The MITA signal is
a Gaussian pulse signal with bandwidth from dc to 150 KHz.
The probing pulses share the same frequency spectrum with the
MITA signal because they share the same sensor array. The full
wave simulations in forward and backward manner are done by
PSTD numerical method [25], [50], [51]. Each full wave simulation will cost around 1 min in 250 √ó 250 mesh grids on Intel
Xeon E5645 workstation and approximately 15 min for each
iteration. Once again our goal of AAM is to provide an updated
velocity model to increase the imaging performance of modelbased inversion. The quality of reconstructed velocity model is
not used as a performance indicator but the quality of tumor
image is.
At first, the tumor transmits a MITA signal and all the sensors
play as receivers to receive the propagated MITA signal. This
is the forward simulation of microwave induced thermoacoustic
signal of tumors. The received signals can be used to reconstruct
the tumor, but without the model information, the image result
is not satisfactory. To get the information of the background
model, the AAM method begins. To better reconstruct the tumor
source, the checkerboard pattern model should be reconstructed
first. The proposed AAM method utilizes acoustic sensors to
transmit external probing pulses to probe and reconstruct the
model. Then the unknown tumor source can be imaged under
the reconstructed model.
In AAM method, first, ten sensors are chosen to play as transmitters (shown as stars in Fig. 4). They will transmit the active
probing pulses, impulse signals from dc to 150 KHz spectrum.
The probing pulses are transmitted into the checkerboard pattern
model one by one in ten forward simulations. All the received
signals are stored as observed signals. This is the active probing
part of the proposed AAM method. Then the adjoint modeling
part of AAM begins with an initial homogeneous model. The
work flow of adjoint modeling is shown in Fig. 3. After each
iteration, the model is updated. The synthetic signals will be
generated by forward simulations under the updated model and
be used to recalculate the kernels by (14) and the adjoint sources
by (8). The traveltime misfit function between the observed signal and updated synthesized signal is also recorded and scaled
to indicate the convergence of the iterations.
Fig. 5(a) shows the updated model after 12 iterations. For
comparison, Fig. 5(b) shows the reconstructed model by the
FM method [37]. Although the AAM and the FM method are
both targeted on minimizing the time-of-flight misfit between
the synthesized signal and the observed signal, it is very obvious
that the AAM method has well reconstructed the four high/low
speed areas, as the checkerboard pattern. For the FM method,
since it uses the tumor source as the only illuminating source,
which leads to the lack of viewing angles and also because of the
ray trace approximation used in FM, it can easily reconstruct the
shape of heterogeneous area as fan-shaped shown in Fig. 5(b),
instead of the original shape of breast structure. Fig. 5(c) shows
the history of scaled misfit function in AAM method. After four
or six iterations, the scale of misfit function has dropped 92%

To verify the noise robustness of the proposed method, the
checkerboard pattern simulation has been repeated with different SNR. As mentioned in Section II, the receivers of active
probing part are the same set of sensors used to receive MITA

signals, which means the noise floor of received probing signals should be the same as MITA signals by sharing the same
sensors, same cables, same preamplifiers and the same digitizers. Usually for MITAT systems published recently, the SNR
of MITA signal is about 14‚Äì18 dB [20], [21], [23]. Considering
the power of active probing signal is significantly larger than the
thermal-acoustic signal, the SNR situation of received probing
signals will be far better than MITA signals. For example, the
Olympus V314-SU sensor can take 20‚Äì80 mW average power
input during pulse transmission, providing hundreds of times
larger compression wave in the medium than thermoacoustic
sources. Considering the transmission losses and the linearity
of high gain preamplifiers, the range of SNR in AAM simulations is set from 17 dB to infinity (no noise) to cover possible
scenarios. For further reference with even lower SNR, the 14

IV. CONCLUSION

Fig. 11. Adjoint modeling and tumor imaging results: (a) ground truth velocity
model of coronal A; (b) AAM updated model after ten iterations; (c) TRM

Aiming to improve the imaging quality of model-based inversion and imaging method for MITAT, an AAM method is
proposed. The main idea of the method is to apply active probing and adjoint modeling method into the scanning and imaging
strategy of MITAT. This method fully utilizes the reciprocity of
acoustic receivers and incorporates the adjoint modeling method
idea into the inversion and imaging procedure. The feasibility
and effectiveness of the method has been demonstrated through
some numerical simulations with a checkerboard pattern model
and anatomical realistic breast models. Numerical simulations
show that the AAM method significantly improves the imaging quality of TRM imaging method by providing models with
structural heterogeneity of breast environment. The robustness
of the AAM method at different SNRs has also been studied.
Compared with the result of model without structural heterogeneity, the AAM has dominant advantage on reconstructing
the shape of tumor sources without extra modeling data from
UT or MRI results. This research results can also be considered
as preresearch to our further studies by experimental MITAT
system [20], [21] combined with artificial phantoms of breast
with structural heterogeneity of acoustic velocity.
