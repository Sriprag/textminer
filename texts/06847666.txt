Abstractâ€”Parametric probability models are common references for channel characterization. However, the limited number
of samples and uncertainty of the propagation scenario affect the
characterization accuracy of parametric models for body area networks. In this paper, we propose a sparse nonparametric probability model for body area wireless channel characterization. The
path loss and root-mean-square delay, which are significant wireless channel parameters, can be learned from this nonparametric
model. A comparison with available parametric models shows that
the proposed model is very feasible for the body area propagation environment and can be seen as a significant supplement to
parametric approaches.

environments and mechanisms include the land environment
[1], mobile satellite links [2], indoor environments [3], outdoor
propagation [4], mobile packet radio networks [5], digital mobile radio channels [6], multiple-user mobile radio [7], microcellular mobile radio systems [8], correlated Nakagami-fading
environments [9], and Rayleigh fading channels [10].
Recently, body-centric wireless communications (BCWCs)
have enjoyed significant attention [11], [12]. Body-centric wireless networks consist of a number of nodes and units placed on

These nodes transmit and relay data between each other or with

Sani et al. [14] characterized the on-body ultrawideband
(UWB) channel, and demonstrated that the Nakagami distribution was the optimal model for the root-mean-square (RMS)
delay. In [15], horn-shaped self-complementary antennae and
planar inverted cone antennae were used as transceivers, with
a log-normal model describing the probability of mean excess
delay and RMS delay. Alomainy et al. [16] investigated cumulative probability models for microstrip patch antennae, and
obtained parameters for a log-normal model. Hall et al. [17]
used a theoretical Rayleigh distribution to approximate the relative power level for the waist (Tx) to right-head (Rx) link, and
this Rayleigh model has proved useful in characterizing indoor
and outdoor propagation channels. In [18], the cumulative distribution functions (CDFs) of both the mean excess delay and
RMS spread delay were derived and compared with a Gaussian
distribution.
Thus, for many propagation environments [1]â€“[10], parametric probability estimation, which will be introduced later, is the
core method for propagation characterization. However, for the
body-centric scenario [11]â€“[18], the presence and characteristics of the human body make the rationality of this propagation
characterization mode debatable, and there is very little in the
literature that discusses this problem. By reviewing relevant
literature, we found that supervised and unsupervised parametric estimation are the two major approaches for characterizing
radio propagation in other environments [1]â€“[10], with parametric methods even used for the on-body scenario [11]â€“[18].
In supervised estimation, known samples are trained to obtain
an optimal model; unsupervised estimation models the sampled
data directly. The sparse nonparametric model described in this
paper is a supervised learning method. The basic foundation for

nonparametric probability estimation was developed by Fix and
Hodges [19], whose results were based on the concept that the
value of a probability function at a continuity point can be estimated using the number of sample observations that fall within
a small region about that point. Some breakthroughs in nonparametric probability estimation have been made, such as the
kernel method [20] and K-nearest-neighbors [21]. However, a
large number of samples are necessary to guarantee the accuracy
of the results in these two approaches, but limited radio propagation samples can be collected in the body-centric scenario.
Against this background, Weston et al. [22] proposed that the
support vector technique of solving linear operator equations
could be applied to the problem of probability estimation. This
would reduce the sample size considerably.
In this paper, a sparsity-inspired nonparametric probability
approximation technique is used to characterize radio propagation in the body-centric scenario. The rest of this paper is
organized as follows. Section II introduces the sparse nonparametric probability approximation technique and the proposed
algorithm. Section III presents some results from the proposed
technique and discusses the specificity of the nonparametric
model. In Section IV, we discuss our conclusions.

D. Summary
In parametric estimation, a specific functional form is assumed for the probability model, and a number of parameters are optimized by fitting the model to the dataset [25].

YANG et al.: SPARSITY-INSPIRED NONPARAMETRIC PROBABILITY CHARACTERIZATION FOR RADIO PROPAGATION IN BODY AREA

However, when the radio propagation occurs around the human
body, there is no mature probability model to follow. Therefore, nonparametric estimation should be introduced. In nonparametric estimation, no specific functional form is assumed,
and the probability distribution is determined entirely by the data
[21], [25]â€“[27]. For example, if X1 , X2 , . . . Xn is a random
1-D sample
 from a continuous probability function f , where
f (x) â‰¥ 0, R f (x) = 1, the general problem is to estimate f
when no formal parametric structure is specified; f is taken to
belong to a large enough family of densities that it cannot be
represented through a finite number of parameters [28]. From
the definition presented above, the nonparametric probability
estimation is in accordance with the actual case of body-centric
wireless channels, which are often affected by factors such as
the human body posture and individual differences.

IV. CONCLUSION
A sparsity-inspired nonparametric model was proposed to
characterize body area channels, which are normally analyzed
by existing parametric probability models. The great strength
of this model is that it is independent of prior knowledge, as it
is difficult to obtain sufficient samples for variable propagation
environments such as the human body. Because of the sparsity
of nonzero coefficients, only limited samples need be collected.
This sample size of the order of 102 [36] is also frequently
used for the characterization of WBANs [37]. Finally, the proposed nonparametric models for the frequency domain and time
domain were compared with existing parametric models. The
results demonstrate that, by adjusting the regression coefficients,
the nonparametric models were able to outperform the parametric methods. Thus, our models are extensively applicable for
variable channel characterization.
