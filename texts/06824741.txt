Ganesh R. Naik, Member, IEEE, and Hung T. Nguyen, Senior Member, IEEE

Abstract‚ÄîSurface electromyography (sEMG) is widely used in
evaluating the functional status of the hand to assist in hand gesture recognition, prosthetics and rehabilitation applications. The
sEMG is a noninvasive, easy to record signal of superficial muscles
from the skin surface. Considering the nonstationary characteristics of sEMG, recent feature selection of hand gesture recognition
using sEMG signals necessitate designers to use nonnegative matrix
factorization (NMF)-based methods. This method exploits both the
additive and sparse nature of signals by extracting accurate and reliable measurements of sEMG features using a minimum number
of sensors. The testing has been conducted for simple and complex finger flexions using several experiments with artificial neural
network classification scheme. It is shown, both by simulation and
experimental studies, that the proposed algorithm is able to classify ten finger flexions (five simple and five complex finger flexions)
recorded from two sEMG sensors up to 92% (95% for simple and
87% for complex flexions) accuracy. The recognition performances
of simple and complex finger flexions are also validated with NMF
permutation matrix analysis.
Index Terms‚ÄîArtificial neural network (ANN), auto regression
(AR), flexions, gestures, hand gesture recognition, nonnegative matrix factorization (NMF), principal component analysis (PCA), root
mean square (RMS), surface electromyography (sEMG).

I. INTRODUCTION
URFACE electromyography (sEMG) is the electrical
recording of the muscle activity from the surface by the
muscle cells when these cells are electrically or neurologically
activated [1], [2]. It is detected from superficial muscles by using surface electrodes, has rich motor control information, and
is closely related to the strength of muscle contraction. Hence,
it is an obvious choice for control of the prosthesis and other
similar applications. In the recent past, myoelectric signals are
extensively used for prosthetic and rehabilitation applications
[3]‚Äì[7]. Researchers have been working extensively on various
technical aspects of myoelectric classification techniques such
as electrode placement [8], [9], selection of proper hand gestures
[6], feature selections [10], and selection of proper classifier algorithms [11]‚Äì[14] to improve the classification rate.

In the recent past, matrix factorization algorithms such as
independent component analysis (ICA), principal component
analysis (PCA), and nonnegative matrix factorization (NMF)
have been used for several pattern recognition and data mining
applications [15]‚Äì[18]. ICA is one of the widely used multivariate data analysis technique for source separation and identification. It has produced effective results not only in general Blind
Source Separation (BSS) problems but also used as a source
separation and identification tool in sEMG gesture analysis [9],
[15]. ICA transforms a multidimensional linear vector data into
statistically independent information (also known as independent components). For ICA algorithm, it is the requirement that
the sources must be linear and highly independent. Sometimes
this assumption may not be practicable, especially when we are
dealing with fewer sensors, hence one need to explore other
matrix factorization methods such as PCA and NMF [19].
PCA is an orthogonal linear feature projection method, used
for dimensionality reduction of data. PCA finds orthogonal
eigenvalues having greatest variance in the data. In general, the
number of principal components is less than the original data.
The PCA is based on the concepts of correlation and covariance.
Hence, in PCA, the assumption being made is that the sources
of interest in the data are uncorrelated both in spatial and temporal domains [20]. In contrast, NMF only requires nonnegative
elements in the mixed data [19].
NMF has been widely used for data mining and pattern recognition applications [21]. However, its applications are not exploited widely in sEMG-based applications. Jiang et al. presented a DOF-wise NMF algorithm to extract the neural control
information from the sEMG signals [22]. Theis and Garcia,
compared the effectiveness of various sparse NMF and BSS
methods for sEMG signal decomposition [23]. Yazama et al.
used NMF and evolutionary method to classify different gestures [24]. Ajiboye and Weir identified various sEMG patterns
using NMF and muscle synergies [25].
In myoelectric prostheses design, it is normally assumed that
the necessary control information can be extracted from the surface myoelectric signal. The choice of an appropriate feature
set is crucial for the classifier. Moreover, it has been acknowledged in the literature that the classification accuracy in a pattern recognition framework is more affected by the choice of the
feature set than by the classification algorithm itself [14], [26].
Hence, in this research study, we propose an NMF-based sEMG
pattern recognition system that is able to accurately classify different finger actions and gestures without requiring a large number of electrodes using back propagation (BP) neural network

III. METHODOLOGY
Most of the previous research studies have presented myoelectric classification using multiple sEMG sensors to achieve
better classification rate [7], [14], [26]. However, usage of multiple sEMG sensors are not practicable for prosthetic and stroke
rehabilitation research, as they may cause inconvenience for the
patients wearing too many sensors on their body. Hence, in this
research study, we propose a myoelectric control scheme for
simple and complex finger flexions using only two sEMG sensors. The schematics diagram of the proposed research study is
shown in Fig. 1, which is composed of sEMG signal collection
part, feature selection part, neural network classification part,
and hand gesture recognition part. The feature selection part
takes the responsibilities for selecting and computing features
of sEMG signals by the use of root mean square (RMS) and auto

A. Data Acquisition and Preprocessing
For this research, we have used the sEMG dataset that were
collected and first utilized by Khushaba et al. [29]. This sEMG
datasets repository contains the sEMG data used for detecting
individual and combined fingers movements using two channels
for ten different finger movements. The sEMG data acquisition
and experimental protocol are described in detail by Khushaba
et al. [29]. The brief description of their data acquisition procedure are explained below: the EMG data were acquired from
eight normally limbed individuals (six males and two females),
recording two channels of EMG from electrodes (Delsys DE
2.x series EMG sensors) placed on the right forearm during
ten finger and hand motions. EMG data were collected from
two muscles: extensor and flexor muscles. Sequences of six
trial sets of ten different classes of motion were performed by
each subject from one day. Each trial set consisted of ten finger
movements.
For this research, we categorized the sEMG finger movements‚Äô data (taken from Khushaba et al. EMG datasets repository [29]) into two classes, they are 1) simple finger movements
and 2) complex finger movements. Simple finger class consisted
of the following finger movements, they are: thumb flexion (TF),
index flexion (IF), middle flexion (MF), ring flexion (RF), little
flexion (LF). The complex finger movements consisted of the
following finger movements: thumb-index flexion (TIF), thumbmiddle flexion (TMF), thumb-ring flexion (TRF), thumb-little
flexion (TLF) and hand close (HC). These data were recorded
for a duration of 5 s each. Shoulder and elbow positions were
fixed with an arm support of the chair for all subjects. Measured surface EMG signals were sampled at 4000 Hz and were
amplified with a gain of 1000 √ó using an EMG measurement
system (Bagnoli Desktop EMG Systems, Del-sys Inc.). Movement artifact (< 20 Hz), power-line interference (50 Hz), and
high-frequency noise (> 450 Hz) were also removed. In total,
960 recordings with a length of 20 000 samples (5 s) were available for analysis (8 subjects √ó 2 muscles √ó 10 motions √ó 6
sets). The overall gesture recognition scheme is shown in Fig. 1.

C. Data Classification and Cross-Validation
Using Neural Network
In order to determine the robustness of the proposed finger
movements‚Äô recognition scheme, the choice of pattern recognition algorithms and statistical methods are highly important.
In the proposed scheme, we used feed-forward BP type ANN
classifier to identify/classify different finger movements. The
ANN configurations are capable of managing useful information from the extracted sEMG features and can represent them
through layers of weighted neurons and biases between the layers [33], [34].
The designed BP neural network consists of three layers, they
are: input, hidden, and output layers, respectively. Apart from
input layer, each of the layers has a weight matrix W, a bias vector b, and an output vector a. The ANN network is optimized
with suitable hidden layers to obtain better classification accuracy. The BP neural network algorithm usually sets the weights
and biases to minimize the mean square error by using gradient
of the performance function [33]. The classification accuracy
of the data features can be computed statistically using crossvalidation method. The training and testing in the BP neural
network architecture are performed with an equal number of
datasets selected randomly for each user.
Cross validation is the statistical method of separating a sample of data into subsets such that the data analysis is initially
carried out on a single subset, whereas the other subset(s) are
reserved for subsequent use in confirming and validating the
initial data analysis. The initial and the other subset(s) of data
are called the training set and the validation or testing sets respectively [34]‚Äì[36]. One of the frequently used cross validation
methods is the holdout method. In this method, the dataset is

481

separated into two mutually exclusive sets, called the training
set and the testing set [34], [37]. Initially, the function approximator fits a function using the training set only and later, the
function approximator is asked to envisage the output values
for the data in the testing set (unknown dataset). The mean absolute test set error is acquired to evaluate the model and for
random selection of training and testing of feature vectors. For
this research, a random subsampling cross-validation method is
used. This method implements data split and each split arbitrarily selects a fixed number of examples without replacement. For
each data split, we retrain the BP neural network classifier from
scratch with the training feature vectors and estimate with the
test feature vectors. The true classification accuracy is achieved
as the average of the separate estimates [34], [36]‚Äì[38].
The BP neural network is trained with several sets of data features that are extracted from the NMF separated sEMG signals
(features from sEMG signals after NMF) for predefined finger
movements. The size of the hidden layer and other parameters
of the network are chosen iteratively after experimentation with
the BP neural network algorithm. These experiments use sigmoid as the threshold function and gradient descent and adaptive
learning with momentum as the training algorithm. Each dataset
consists of an input feature vector attained from a particular type
of finger flexion and its matching output vector.
For each of the training sessions, the weights and biases for input hidden layers are saved and used in the next training session
iteratively until a satisfactory simulation results are achieved. A
learning rate of 0.05 and the default momentum rate are found
to be suitable for stable learning of the network. The training
stops when the network converges and the network error is less
than the target error. The weights and biases of the network are
saved and used for testing the network. The data are divided
into training, validation, and test subsets. One-fourth of the data
are used for the validation set (25%), one-fourth for the test
set (25%), and one-half for the training set (50%). The RMS
and AR sEMG features are the inputs to the BP neural network
classifier. The outputs of the BP neural network classifiers are
the different simple and complex finger movements. The classification performance is quantified by the overall Classification

Given the success of utilizing sEMG signals in decoding the
intended wrist and finger movements, there has been significant
research done to achieve more dexterous individual finger control using both high- and low-density EMG electrodes. Recently,
researchers have used high-density sEMG electrodes to identify
different hand and finger movements [30], [39]‚Äì[41]. Highdensity EMG is a noninvasive technique that collects sEMG
signals from many closely spaced electrodes. One of the advantages of this arrangement is that the information collected
from these electrodes can be used to create topographic maps
at each electrode location and to examine the muscle activity patterns during different hand and finger movement tasks.
Moreover, processing of sEMG signals using high-density electrodes provides a quantification of both the temporal and spatial
characteristics of the electric muscle activity. However, a reduction in the number of electrodes, without compromising the
classification accuracy, would significantly simplify the requirements for controlling state-of-the-art prostheses. For instance,
Andrews et al. reported a unique technique targeting the optimal electrode locations for EMG based finger control using
fewer number of sensors [42]. In another study, Khushaba et al.
proposed a novel technique to identify finger and hand movements using only two electrodes [29]. In most recent study, Naik
et al. proposed ICA-based signal processing technique for optimal electrode configuration using only a very few electrodes
[9]. This research study demonstrated higher classification rate
using an NMF-based pattern recognition system. Moreover, an
NMF-based system requires only two sets of bipolar electrodes
that can be easily mounted by the user and trained using ten
examples of the actions (five simple and five complex actions).
Higher classification results of simple gestures also demonstrate
that such a system is easy to train and employ, making it suitable
for use by an individual without expert assistance.
V. CONCLUSION
In this paper, classification of simple and complex finger
flexion movements based on NMF and neural network classifier
is proposed. The sparseness and additive patterns of NMF are
adopted to extract simple and complex features of sEMG signals. Backpropagation ANN is used for hand gesture recognition
based on feature values of sEMG signals. Finally, the robustness of NMF is evaluated using permutation matrix analysis.
The hand gesture recognition model, based on sEMG signals by
using the NMF and ANN, is a novel sEMG gesture recognition
method. It can adapt to the additive and nonstationary characteristic of sEMG and can increase the recognition accuracy of
sEMG actions that are recorded from two sEMG sensors.
The proposed research study has several potential applications in prosthetics, for the elderly and week people with special
needs. The usage of small number of electrodes makes prosthetic
control easy, where the EMG electrodes may be placed above
the amputation point. Moreover, such a system also gives both
amputees and disabled a natural and seamless means of commands and helps in controlling devices. However, at present,
this system has only been tested with able-bodied individuals
and there is a need for testing the same on subjects who have suf-

fered amputations to test the efficacy of this technique. Though
this research study reports experiments conducted for simple
and complex finger flexions, this technique is also suitable for
the analysis of other complex actions and hand postures, where
cross-talk due to overlapping adjacent muscles is an issue.
In conclusion, the sparseness and additive features of the
NMF method can obtain multiple characteristics of sEMG signal and classify the corresponding actions better. Moreover,
NMF is able to identify the complex nature of EMG signals
using minimum number of sEMG sensors (recorded from two
sEMG sensors), which is very useful for prosthetics and rehabilitation applications. Currently, work is underway to compare
the proposed research study with the conventional myoelectric
pattern recognition methods in terms of feature extraction, computational cost, and classification accuracy. Our future work
will concentrate on challenges toward implementing a practical sEMG-based gesture recognition system for amputees and
stroke survivors. These include determining acceptable electrode locations, cross-talk removal, extracting substantial motor
control information, optimizing electrode configurations, and
dealing with the challenges of the sEMG recording in a dynamic environment.
